# Pregnancy Warning Signs

During pregnancy, some symptoms are normal. But certain symptoms can be a sign of a serious problem. Knowing the difference can help you protect yourself and your baby.

## Call 911 or Go to the Emergency Room Immediately If You Have:

- Severe chest pain or shortness of breath that does not go away
- Seizures
- Thoughts of hurting yourself or your baby
- Heavy vaginal bleeding (soaking more than one pad per hour)
- Severe allergic reaction (difficulty breathing, swelling of face/throat)
- High fever (over 100.4°F / 38°C) with chills

## Call Your Provider Right Away If You Have:

### Possible Signs of Preeclampsia
- Headache that does not go away or gets worse
- Vision changes (blurred vision, seeing spots or flashing lights, sensitivity to light)
- Sudden swelling of your face, hands, or feet
- Pain in your upper right abdomen (under the ribs)
- Nausea or vomiting in the second half of pregnancy
- Rapid weight gain (more than 3-5 pounds in a week)
- Decreased urine output

### Possible Signs of Preterm Labor
- Regular contractions before 37 weeks (tightening of the uterus that happens every 10 minutes or more)
- Change in vaginal discharge (becomes watery, mucus-like, or bloody)
- Pelvic pressure (feeling that baby is pushing down)
- Low, dull backache that doesn't go away
- Abdominal cramps with or without diarrhea

### Possible Signs of Placental Problems
- Vaginal bleeding at any time during pregnancy
- Severe abdominal pain
- Sudden decrease in baby's movement

### Signs of Infection
- Fever over 100.4°F (38°C)
- Painful urination or burning sensation
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge
- Chills or body aches

### Other Concerning Symptoms
- Baby is not moving as much as usual (fewer than 10 movements in 2 hours during kick counts)
- Persistent vomiting (cannot keep fluids down)
- Severe abdominal pain or cramping
- Fluid leaking or gushing from your vagina (could indicate ruptured membranes)
- Dizziness or fainting
- Persistent itching all over your body (may indicate a liver condition called cholestasis)

## When in Doubt, Call

If you are unsure whether a symptom is serious, call your provider. It is always better to call and have it turn out to be nothing than to wait when something is wrong. Your provider expects these calls and would rather hear from you than have you worry at home.

## How to Describe Your Symptoms

When you call your provider, be ready to tell them:
1. What symptoms you are experiencing
2. When they started
3. How severe they are (on a scale of 1-10 for pain)
4. Whether they are constant or come and go
5. What makes them better or worse
6. How far along you are in your pregnancy
7. Any medications you have taken

## Clinical Language Guide
- "I have a terrible headache" → "I have a severe, persistent headache unrelieved by rest or acetaminophen"
- "I'm seeing stars" → "I'm experiencing visual disturbances (scotomata, photopsia)"
- "I feel tightening in my belly" → "I'm having uterine contractions" (note how often and how long they last)
- "I'm leaking fluid" → "I may have ruptured membranes / premature rupture of membranes (PROM)"
- "I'm bleeding" → "I'm experiencing vaginal bleeding" (describe amount: spotting, light, heavy, with clots)
- "The baby isn't moving" → "I've noticed decreased fetal movement"
- "I feel pressure down low" → "I'm experiencing pelvic pressure that may indicate cervical changes"
- "It hurts under my ribs on the right" → "I have right upper quadrant or epigastric pain"
- "I can't stop throwing up" → "I'm experiencing hyperemesis (persistent vomiting) and unable to maintain hydration"
