# Pregnancy Loss and Miscarriage

A miscarriage is the loss of a pregnancy before 20 weeks. It is also sometimes called a spontaneous abortion or early pregnancy loss. Miscarriage is very common. About 10-20% of known pregnancies end in miscarriage, and the actual number is likely higher because many miscarriages happen very early, before a woman even knows she is pregnant.

## Causes

Most miscarriages happen because the fetus is not developing normally. Chromosome problems account for about half of early miscarriages. These problems are usually not related to the mother's or father's health. Other causes may include infections, hormonal problems, uterine or cervical problems, or chronic health conditions like uncontrolled diabetes or thyroid disease.

Most miscarriages cannot be prevented. They are NOT caused by exercise, stress, sex, working, or lifting things. It is very important to know that miscarriage is not your fault.

## Warning Signs

Contact your health care provider if you experience: vaginal bleeding (ranging from light spotting to heavy bleeding), cramping or pain in your lower abdomen or back, tissue or fluid passing from your vagina, or a sudden decrease in pregnancy symptoms.

Note: Some light spotting or cramping can be normal in early pregnancy. But it is always best to let your provider know.

## What Happens

If a miscarriage is suspected, your provider may order: a pelvic exam to check if your cervix has dilated, an ultrasound to check for a heartbeat and fetal development, blood tests to check pregnancy hormone (hCG) levels, and tissue tests if tissue has passed.

## Types of Miscarriage

Threatened miscarriage: You have bleeding but your cervix hasn't opened. The pregnancy may continue.
Inevitable miscarriage: You have bleeding and your cervix is opening. The pregnancy cannot continue.
Incomplete miscarriage: Some pregnancy tissue has passed but some remains in the uterus.
Complete miscarriage: All pregnancy tissue has passed from the uterus.
Missed miscarriage: The embryo has died but has not passed from the uterus. You may not have symptoms.

## Treatment

Treatment depends on the type of miscarriage. Options may include: watchful waiting (letting the tissue pass naturally), medication to help the tissue pass, or a procedure called dilation and curettage (D&C) to remove remaining tissue.

## Emotional Impact

Losing a pregnancy can cause intense grief, sadness, and feelings of loss. These are normal. About 20% of women who experience a miscarriage develop symptoms of depression and/or anxiety. For some, these symptoms can persist for one to three years. It is important to give yourself time to grieve and to seek support.

You may experience: sadness, guilt (even though it is not your fault), anger, difficulty sleeping, loss of appetite, difficulty concentrating, or anxiety about future pregnancies.

## Getting Support

Talk to your provider about your feelings. Talk to your partner, family, or friends. Consider joining a pregnancy loss support group. Seek professional counseling if your grief feels overwhelming or lasts a long time. Know that it is okay to grieve in your own way and at your own pace.

## Future Pregnancies

Most women who have had one miscarriage go on to have successful pregnancies. Having one miscarriage does not mean you will have another. If you have had two or more miscarriages in a row (recurrent pregnancy loss), your provider may recommend additional testing to look for underlying causes.

## Clinical Language Guide
- "I'm bleeding and I'm scared" → "I'm experiencing vaginal bleeding at [X] weeks of pregnancy" (describe amount and color)
- "I feel like something is wrong" → "I have concerns about the viability of my pregnancy"
- "I had a miscarriage before and I'm worried it will happen again" → "I have a history of pregnancy loss and would like to discuss monitoring for this pregnancy"
- "I'm not feeling pregnant anymore" → "My pregnancy symptoms have suddenly decreased or disappeared"
- "I passed something from my vagina" → "I've passed tissue vaginally" (save tissue if possible for your provider to examine)
- "I feel really sad about losing the baby" → "I'm grieving my pregnancy loss and may need emotional support or counseling"
- "I feel guilty, like it was my fault" → "I'm experiencing guilt related to my miscarriage" (your provider can reassure you it was not your fault)
