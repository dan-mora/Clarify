# Postpartum Care and Breastfeeding

The postpartum period begins after delivery and lasts about 6 to 8 weeks. During this time, your body is recovering from pregnancy and delivery while you are also adjusting to life with a new baby.

## Physical Recovery After Delivery

### Vaginal Delivery Recovery
You may experience: vaginal soreness and swelling, bleeding and discharge (lochia) for up to 6 weeks, cramping as your uterus shrinks back to normal size, discomfort from stitches if you had a tear or episiotomy, hemorrhoids, and breast engorgement.

### C-Section Recovery
In addition to the above, you may have: incision pain and soreness, difficulty moving around, gas and bloating, and a longer overall recovery (typically 6-8 weeks before returning to normal activities).

### When to Call Your Provider After Delivery
- Heavy bleeding (soaking more than one pad per hour)
- Fever over 100.4°F (38°C)
- Severe headache or vision changes (may indicate postpartum preeclampsia)
- Pain, redness, or swelling in your legs
- Difficulty breathing or chest pain
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge
- Incision that is red, swollen, or draining (C-section)
- Painful urination or difficulty urinating
- Feelings of sadness, hopelessness, or thoughts of harming yourself or your baby

## Breastfeeding

Breast milk provides ideal nutrition for your baby and helps protect against infections and diseases. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends exclusive breastfeeding for about the first 6 months.

### Getting Started
- Try to breastfeed within the first hour after birth (skin-to-skin contact helps)
- Newborns typically feed 8-12 times per day
- Feed on demand (whenever baby shows hunger cues)
- Proper latch is important — the baby should take in the nipple and a large portion of the areola

### Common Breastfeeding Challenges
- Sore or cracked nipples (often caused by incorrect latch)
- Engorgement (breasts are overly full, hard, and painful)
- Low milk supply concerns
- Mastitis (breast infection — symptoms include fever, flu-like symptoms, and a red, painful area on the breast)
- Difficulty with latch
- Tongue-tie in the baby

### Getting Help
- Ask for a lactation consultant at the hospital
- Many community health centers have lactation support
- WIC provides breastfeeding peer counselors
- La Leche League offers free support groups

### If You Cannot or Choose Not to Breastfeed
Formula feeding is a safe and healthy alternative. Talk to your provider about the right formula for your baby. WIC also provides formula support.

## Postpartum Visit

Your postpartum checkup (usually at 6 weeks, but may be earlier) is important. Your provider will: check your physical recovery, screen for postpartum depression, discuss birth control options, address any breastfeeding concerns, update your immunizations if needed, and discuss a care plan for any ongoing health conditions.

## Self-Care Tips

- Rest when the baby rests
- Accept help from family and friends
- Eat nutritious meals
- Stay hydrated (especially if breastfeeding)
- Gentle walking when you feel ready
- Wait for your provider's clearance before returning to exercise or sexual activity
- It's normal for your body to look and feel different — be patient with yourself

## Clinical Language Guide
- "I'm bleeding a lot since delivery" → "I'm experiencing heavy postpartum bleeding (lochia)" (describe amount — how many pads per hour)
- "My stitches hurt" → "I'm having perineal pain at my laceration repair site"
- "My breasts are rock hard and painful" → "I'm experiencing breast engorgement"
- "It hurts when the baby latches" → "I'm having pain during breastfeeding, possibly related to latch"
- "I have a red, hot spot on my breast with fever" → "I may have mastitis — I have a localized area of redness, warmth, and tenderness with fever"
- "The baby won't latch" → "I'm having difficulty establishing breastfeeding and would like lactation support"
- "I don't feel like myself since the baby" → "I'm experiencing mood changes since delivery that concern me" (see postpartum depression document)
- "My C-section incision looks red" → "I'm concerned about my surgical incision — it appears erythematous (red) with possible signs of infection"
