# Labor and Delivery

## Signs That Labor Is Starting

As you get closer to your due date, watch for these signs that labor may begin soon:

### Early Signs (Days to Weeks Before)
- "Lightening" — the baby drops lower into your pelvis, and you may feel like you can breathe more easily
- Increased vaginal discharge or mucus plug (a thick plug of mucus that seals the cervix during pregnancy)
- Nesting instinct — a burst of energy and desire to prepare for the baby
- Braxton Hicks contractions may become more frequent (these are practice contractions that are irregular and go away with rest)

### Signs Labor Has Begun
- Regular contractions that get closer together, stronger, and longer over time
- Contractions that do not go away when you change position, walk, or rest
- Your water breaks (rupture of membranes) — a gush or steady leak of fluid from your vagina
- Lower back pain that doesn't go away
- Bloody show — a small amount of blood mixed with mucus from the vagina

## When to Go to the Hospital

Call your provider or go to the hospital when:
- Contractions are about 5 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute each, for at least 1 hour (the "5-1-1 rule")
- Your water breaks (note the time and color of the fluid — clear is normal; green or brown may indicate meconium and you should go immediately)
- You have heavy vaginal bleeding
- You cannot walk or talk during contractions
- You have any concerns at all

## Stages of Labor

### First Stage: Early and Active Labor
Early labor: Cervix dilates from 0 to 6 centimeters. Contractions are mild to moderate, 5-30 minutes apart. This can last hours to days, especially for first pregnancies.

Active labor: Cervix dilates from 6 to 10 centimeters. Contractions are stronger, closer together (2-5 minutes apart), and last 45-60 seconds. This is when most women go to the hospital.

Transition: The final part of active labor. Cervix dilates from 8 to 10 centimeters. Contractions are very strong, 2-3 minutes apart. You may feel nausea, pressure, and an urge to push.

### Second Stage: Pushing and Delivery
Once your cervix is fully dilated (10 centimeters), you will begin pushing. Your provider will guide you. This stage can last from minutes to several hours. The baby is born.

### Third Stage: Placenta Delivery
After the baby is born, you will deliver the placenta. This usually happens within 5-30 minutes. Your provider may press on your abdomen and you may have mild contractions.

## Pain Management Options

Discuss your preferences with your provider before labor. Options include:

Non-medication approaches: breathing techniques, relaxation, position changes, walking, warm baths or showers, massage, birthing ball.

Medications: epidural block (most common — numbs the lower body), spinal block, IV pain medication, local anesthesia for specific areas.

You can change your mind about pain management at any time during labor.

## Cesarean Delivery (C-Section)

A cesarean delivery is surgery to deliver the baby through an incision in the abdomen and uterus. It may be planned (scheduled) or unplanned (emergency). Reasons for a C-section include: the baby is in a breech position, the baby is too large, labor is not progressing, the baby is in distress, placenta problems, or the mother has certain health conditions.

## Birth Plan

Talk with your provider about what to include in your birth plan: how you want to manage pain during labor, who you want in the room with you, your preferences for the baby after birth (skin-to-skin contact, breastfeeding), cord blood banking decisions, and any cultural or religious practices.

## Clinical Language Guide
- "I keep having pains that come and go" → "I'm experiencing intermittent contractions" (note frequency, duration, intensity)
- "The pain is regular now, every 5 minutes" → "I'm having regular contractions at 5-minute intervals"
- "I think my water broke" → "I believe I've had a rupture of membranes" (note time and fluid color)
- "I saw some blood and mucus" → "I've had bloody show"
- "I feel like I need to push" → "I'm feeling rectal/bearing-down pressure and an urge to push"
- "I want the epidural" → "I'd like to discuss epidural anesthesia for pain management"
- "I don't want a C-section" → "I'd like to discuss my preferences for vaginal delivery and the circumstances that would require a cesarean"
- "I feel like labor isn't moving" → "I'm concerned about my labor progression — can you check my cervical dilation?"
