# Common Symptoms During Pregnancy

Growing a baby is hard work. Your body will go through a lot of changes as your baby grows and your hormones change. Along with the aches and pains of pregnancy, you will feel other new or changing symptoms.

## Fatigue
Being tired is common during pregnancy. Most women feel tired the first few months, then again toward the end. Exercise, rest, and a proper diet can make you feel less tired. It may also help to take rest breaks or naps every day.

## Frequent Urination
Early on in the pregnancy, you will likely be making more trips to the bathroom. As your uterus grows and rises higher in your abdomen, the need to urinate often may lessen. Even so, you will continue to urinate more throughout pregnancy. As you get closer to delivery and your baby descends into your pelvis, you will need to urinate much more, and the amount of urine passed at one time will be less. If you have pain when you urinate or a change in urine odor or color, contact your health care provider. These could be signs of a bladder infection. Some pregnant women also leak urine when they cough or sneeze.

## Vaginal Discharge
You may see more vaginal discharge while pregnant. Contact your provider if the discharge has a foul odor, has a greenish color, makes you feel itchy, or causes pain or soreness. Certain vaginal infections are important to treat during pregnancy, as they may cause preterm labor.

## Constipation
Having a harder time than usual moving your bowels is normal during pregnancy. Hormone changes during pregnancy slow down your digestive system. Later in your pregnancy, the pressure from your uterus on your rectum may also worsen the problem. You can ease constipation by eating raw fruits and vegetables for extra fiber, eating whole grain or bran cereals, using a fiber supplement regularly, and drinking plenty of water (8 to 9 cups daily). Ask your provider about trying a stool softener. Also ask before using laxatives during pregnancy.

## Heartburn
While you are pregnant, food stays in your stomach and bowels longer. This may cause heartburn (stomach acid moving back up into the esophagus). You can reduce heartburn by eating small meals, avoiding spicy and greasy foods, not drinking large amounts of liquid before bedtime, not exercising for at least 2 hours after you eat, and not lying down flat right after a meal.

## Nosebleeds and Bleeding Gums
Some women have nose and gum bleeding while they are pregnant. This is because the tissues in their nose and gums get dry, and the blood vessels dilate and are closer to the surface. Drink lots of fluids, get lots of vitamin C, use a humidifier, and brush your teeth with a soft toothbrush.

## Swelling, Varicose Veins, and Hemorrhoids
Swelling in your legs is common. You may see more swelling as you get closer to giving birth. The swelling is caused by your uterus pressing on the veins. To reduce leg swelling, raise your legs and rest your feet on a surface higher than your belly. Lie on your side in bed (lying on the left side is better if you can do it comfortably). Wear support pantyhose or compression stockings. Limit salty foods.

IMPORTANT: Leg swelling that occurs with headaches or high blood pressure can be a sign of a serious medical complication of pregnancy called preeclampsia. It is important to discuss leg swelling with your provider.

## Breathing Problems
Some women feel short of breath at times while they are pregnant. It happens more often in the early part of the pregnancy due to the changes in your hormones. It may also happen again toward the end of your pregnancy because of pressure from the baby. Mild shortness of breath from exercise that quickly gets better is not serious.

EMERGENCY: Severe chest pain or shortness of breath that does not go away can be a sign of a serious medical complication. Call 911 or go to an emergency room right away if you have these symptoms.

## Clinical Language Guide
When describing these symptoms to your provider, here are helpful clinical terms:
- "I feel tired all the time" → "I'm experiencing persistent fatigue"
- "I keep going to the bathroom" → "I have increased urinary frequency"
- "I have discharge" → "I've noticed changes in vaginal discharge" (describe color, odor, consistency)
- "I can't go to the bathroom" → "I'm experiencing constipation"
- "My stomach burns after eating" → "I'm having heartburn or acid reflux"
- "My legs are puffy" → "I have swelling (edema) in my lower extremities"
- "I can't catch my breath" → "I'm experiencing shortness of breath (dyspnea)"
- "My nose keeps bleeding" → "I'm having recurrent epistaxis (nosebleeds)"
