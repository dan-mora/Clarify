Topic: Social Support Resources for Pregnant Women

# Social Support Resources

Pregnancy involves more than medical care. Many women need support with food, housing, transportation, mental health, and other basic needs. Your health center can help connect you to resources. You are not alone, and asking for help is a sign of strength.

## Food and Nutrition Support

### WIC (Women, Infants, and Children)
WIC is a federal nutrition program for pregnant women, new mothers, and young children. WIC provides: healthy foods (fruits, vegetables, whole grains, dairy, eggs, peanut butter), nutrition education, breastfeeding support, and referrals to health care. To qualify, you need to be pregnant, postpartum, or breastfeeding with an income at or below 185% of the federal poverty level. Many women who use community health centers qualify. Ask your clinic about WIC enrollment.

### SNAP (Food Stamps)
The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program provides monthly benefits for buying food. Pregnant women may qualify for expedited enrollment. Apply through your county social services office.

### Food Banks
Local food banks and food pantries provide free groceries. Many have special programs for pregnant women. Ask your clinic's social worker for locations near you.

## Housing Support

If you are experiencing housing instability or homelessness, tell your provider. They can connect you to: emergency shelter programs, transitional housing for pregnant women and families, housing assistance programs (Section 8/Housing Choice Vouchers), and local housing authorities.

Many communities have shelters specifically for pregnant women and families. Your clinic's social worker can help you access these resources.

## Transportation to Medical Appointments

Getting to prenatal visits is critical. Options that may be available to you include: Medicaid transportation benefits (many states provide free rides to medical appointments for Medicaid recipients), medical center shuttle services, community transportation programs, rideshare voucher programs, and public transit passes through social services.

Tell your provider if transportation is a barrier to attending your prenatal visits. Many health centers have dedicated staff to help arrange transportation.

## Health Insurance and Financial Assistance

### Medicaid for Pregnant Women
Medicaid covers pregnant women with higher income limits than regular Medicaid. Coverage includes prenatal care, delivery, postpartum care, and the baby's first year of life. Apply through your state's Medicaid office or healthcare.gov. Many community health centers have enrollment counselors who can help.

### Sliding Fee Scale
Federally qualified health centers (FQHCs) are required to see you regardless of your ability to pay. They use a sliding fee scale based on your income. You will never be turned away.

### Presumptive Eligibility
Many states offer presumptive eligibility, which means you can get Medicaid coverage immediately if you are pregnant, even before your full application is processed.

## Mental Health Support

### National Maternal Mental Health Hotline
Call 1-833-943-5746 (1-833-9-HELP4MOMS). Free and confidential support available 24/7 in English, Spanish, and 60+ other languages.

### 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline
Call or text 988 for immediate crisis support. Available 24/7.

### Counseling Services
Many community health centers offer behavioral health services including counseling for depression, anxiety, stress, and domestic violence. Ask your provider for a referral.

## Domestic Violence Resources

If you are in an unsafe relationship, your provider can help. Everything you tell your provider is confidential.

### National Domestic Violence Hotline
Call 1-800-799-7233 (1-800-799-SAFE) or text START to 88788. Available 24/7 with interpreters for 200+ languages.

Safety planning, emergency shelter referrals, legal advocacy, and counseling are available.

## Substance Use Support

If you are using alcohol, tobacco, or other substances during pregnancy, your provider can help without judgment. Treatment is available and can make a real difference for you and your baby. Ask your provider about: smoking cessation programs, substance use treatment designed for pregnant women, medication-assisted treatment if appropriate, and support groups.

## Childcare and Parenting Support

Resources may include: home visiting programs (like Healthy Start, Nurse-Family Partnership), parenting classes, car seat programs, baby supply assistance (diapers, formula, clothing), and breastfeeding support (lactation consultants, La Leche League).

## How to Ask for Help

If you need any of these resources, here are ways to start the conversation:
- "I'm having trouble affording food" → "I'd like help enrolling in WIC or other food assistance programs"
- "I don't have a safe place to stay" → "I need housing assistance — can you connect me with a social worker?"
- "I can't get to my appointments" → "I need help with transportation to my prenatal visits"
- "I don't have insurance" → "I need help with Medicaid enrollment or financial assistance"
- "I'm not feeling safe at home" → "I'd like to speak with someone privately about my home situation"
- "I'm struggling with substance use" → "I'd like to discuss treatment options that are safe for pregnancy"
- "I'm feeling really overwhelmed" → "I think I need mental health support"
- "I don't know how I'm going to take care of a baby" → "I'd like to learn about parenting resources and support programs"

Remember: Your health center is here to help with ALL of these needs, not just medical care. Social workers, case managers, and enrollment counselors are part of your care team.
