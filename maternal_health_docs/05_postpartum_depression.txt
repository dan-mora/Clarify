# Postpartum Depression

Postpartum depression is a common but serious mood disorder that can begin anytime within the first year after childbirth. Without treatment, symptoms may make it hard to care for your baby or manage daily tasks. It is different from the "baby blues," which usually go away within a couple of weeks after birth.

## Baby Blues vs. Postpartum Depression

Baby blues are very common (up to 70% of new parents experience them). They start about three days after childbirth and include feeling sad, weepy, or anxious. These feelings tend to come and go and usually get better on their own within a week or two.

Postpartum depression is more intense and lasts longer. Symptoms may last for months and do not get better without treatment. About 10-15% of women experience postpartum depression.

## Causes

The exact cause is unknown, but postpartum depression likely results from a combination of factors including: sudden changes in hormone levels that happen after pregnancy (estrogen and progesterone drop dramatically), lack of sleep, stress from new routines, and a personal or family history of depression.

## Risk Factors

You might be at higher risk if you: have a history of depression, bipolar disorder, or a family history of these conditions, had a multiple birth (twins, triplets, or more), gave birth while in your teens, had health problems during pregnancy, had preterm labor or other complications during childbirth, have a baby with special needs, have ever experienced domestic violence, are dealing with financial stress, had an unplanned pregnancy, lack support from family, partner, and friends.

## Symptoms

Symptoms of postpartum depression include: feeling sad or empty most of the time, eating too much or too little, sleeping too much or too little, crying more than usual or for no clear reason, feeling unusually angry, pulling away from family and friends, feeling worried or anxious often, not having any interest in your baby, constant doubts about your ability to care for your baby, and thinking about hurting yourself or your baby.

If these symptoms begin during pregnancy and continue after childbirth, it is called perinatal depression.

## Screening

Your provider may diagnose postpartum depression using: screening questions or questionnaires (such as the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale) about your mood, sleep, and thoughts; a clinical evaluation based on your symptoms; and blood tests if needed to check for physical conditions such as thyroid disorders that can cause or worsen depression.

## Treatment

Treatments may include: counseling (talk therapy), antidepressant medicines, or a combination of both. Women with milder depression may get better with counseling alone. Tell your provider if you are breastfeeding so they can choose the safest treatment options.

Things that may help at home: ask for help with caring for the baby and household chores, eat well, get exercise every day, get as much sleep as possible, get support from family and friends.

## Postpartum Psychosis

In rare cases, a woman may have a severe form of depression called postpartum psychosis. This is an emergency because it can quickly get worse and put her or others in danger.

## Resources

National Maternal Mental Health Hotline: Free and confidential access to trained counselors, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, in English, Spanish, and more than 60 other languages.

## Clinical Language Guide
When describing these feelings to your provider:
- "I just don't feel like myself" → "I'm experiencing mood changes since delivery that concern me"
- "I cry all the time for no reason" → "I'm having frequent crying episodes without clear triggers"
- "I can't sleep even when the baby sleeps" → "I'm experiencing insomnia despite fatigue, which may indicate depression"
- "I don't feel connected to my baby" → "I'm having difficulty bonding with my infant"
- "I feel like a bad mother" → "I'm experiencing persistent feelings of inadequacy about my parenting"
- "I get angry over little things" → "I'm having irritability and mood lability beyond what feels normal"
- "I keep thinking something bad will happen" → "I'm experiencing intrusive anxious thoughts"
- "I sometimes think my baby would be better off without me" → "I'm having thoughts of hopelessness about my role as a parent" (TELL YOUR PROVIDER IMMEDIATELY)

CRISIS: If you have thoughts of hurting yourself or your baby, call 911, the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, or the National Maternal Mental Health Hotline immediately.
