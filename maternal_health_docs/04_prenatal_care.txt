# Prenatal Care

Getting good care before, during, and after your pregnancy is very important. It can help your baby grow and develop and keep you both healthy. It is the best way to be sure your little one gets a head start on a healthy life.

## Choosing a Provider

You will want to choose a provider for your pregnancy and childbirth. This provider will provide prenatal care, delivery, and postpartum services. Types of providers include obstetricians (OB/GYN), family medicine doctors, certified nurse-midwives, and maternal-fetal medicine specialists (for high-risk pregnancies).

## Visit Schedule

Usually, visits during your pregnancy occur: once a month during weeks 4 through 28, twice a month during weeks 28 through 36, and weekly from week 36 until delivery. If you are over 35 years old or your pregnancy is high risk because of health problems like diabetes or high blood pressure, your provider will probably want to see you more often.

## What Happens at Prenatal Visits

During prenatal visits, your provider will check your health and carefully monitor your pregnancy. Your first prenatal visit may take a little longer. Your provider will most likely: ask about your health history including prior pregnancies, do a physical exam, check your weight and blood pressure, test your blood and urine, calculate your due date, and discuss what to expect during pregnancy.

## Prenatal Tests

Tests during pregnancy may include: ultrasound tests to see how your baby is growing and help establish a due date, blood glucose tests to check for gestational diabetes, blood tests to check for normal fetal DNA, tests to check for sexually transmitted infections (such as syphilis, hepatitis B, and HIV), and depending on your family history you may choose to screen for genetic problems.

## First Trimester (Weeks 1-12)

During early visits your provider will confirm your pregnancy, estimate your due date, start prenatal vitamins (especially folic acid), order initial blood work, and discuss any medications you are taking. You should take a supplement with at least 600 micrograms (0.6 mg) of folic acid every day. Taking folic acid will decrease the risk for certain birth defects.

## Second Trimester (Weeks 13-28)

You will have the glucose challenge test for gestational diabetes (usually between weeks 24-28). Your provider will monitor your baby's growth with fundal height measurements and may order an anatomy scan ultrasound around week 20. You may start feeling the baby move (quickening) around weeks 16-20.

## Third Trimester (Weeks 29-40)

Visits become more frequent. Your provider will check your baby's position, monitor for signs of preeclampsia, do a Group B strep test (around weeks 35-37), and discuss your birth plan. Talk with your provider about what to expect during labor and delivery.

## Vaccines During Pregnancy

Your provider will discuss immunizations and vaccines that are important during pregnancy. There are many safe vaccines that can benefit both you and your baby.

## Things to Avoid During Pregnancy

To help keep yourself and your baby healthy: avoid tobacco, alcohol, and drugs; always check with your provider before starting or stopping any medicine (including over-the-counter medicines and dietary or herbal supplements); be careful with certain foods (raw fish, unpasteurized dairy, deli meats).

## Clinical Language Guide
When talking to your provider about prenatal care:
- "I don't know what vitamins to take" → "Can you recommend a prenatal vitamin regimen?"
- "I'm not sure about my due date" → "Can we confirm my estimated date of delivery (EDD)?"
- "I haven't felt the baby move" → "I'm concerned about decreased fetal movement"
- "I want to know if my baby is okay" → "Can we discuss fetal monitoring options?"
- "I'm scared about labor" → "I'd like to discuss my birth plan and pain management options"
- "I have questions about tests" → "Can you explain the recommended prenatal screening and diagnostic tests?"
- "I'm taking medicine for [condition]" → "I need to review my current medications for pregnancy safety"
